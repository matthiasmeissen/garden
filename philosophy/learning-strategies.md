# Learning Strategies

[tags: #philosophy #learning #epistemology]

How to approach a new topic so that time spent turns into real capability rather than a feeling of being informed.

## Foundational Texts First

Start with canonical sources, not blog posts. Old books carry less algorithmic bias and tend to explain from first principles rather than from whatever framing is currently fashionable. Wikipedia bibliographies, practitioner recommendations, and archive.org are faster routes to these than a search engine. The goal at this stage is not to read everything — it is to locate the two or three anchor texts a field keeps pointing back to.

See [[finding-good-information|Finding Good Information]] for the full approach to sourcing.

## Make Early, Before You Feel Ready

Consuming material about a topic is not the same as learning it. Reading ten articles on DSP does not make you able to write a filter. The move is to start building as soon as a single specific project is possible — even badly, even wrong — and use reading as reference for specific questions that come up in practice.

Waiting until you "feel ready" is a trap. The feeling of readiness arrives only after making, never before. The first attempt reveals which questions are real and which were imagined; that is information you cannot get any other way.

## Triangulate

Do not trust a single source. Read two or three anchors in different formats and cross-check — see [[finding-good-information|Finding Good Information]] for the full approach.

## Use Cross-References Between Domains

Topics connect in unexpected ways. DSP concepts show up in shaders. Woodworking joinery ideas show up in PCB layout. Notice these connections and follow them — a concept understood from two angles sticks in a way that a concept understood from one does not.

## Develop Taste Through Practice

No amount of reading will give you taste — the ability to tell a good approach from a plausible-sounding one. Taste comes from making enough things to have preferences, and from having preferences broken by running into reality. Protect time for practice the way you would protect time for reading; it is not a lower form of engagement with a topic.

## The Cycle

In short: find the foundational texts, start making, let practice generate specific questions, return to the texts for answers, triangulate across sources, repeat. The loop is what produces understanding. Consumption alone, however well-sourced, does not.
