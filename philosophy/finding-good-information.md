# Finding Good Information

[tags: #philosophy #knowledge-management #epistemology]

Finding foundational, actionable knowledge on the open internet is harder than it should be, and the difficulty is structural rather than accidental. Understanding the structure is the first step to working around it.

## The Incentive Problem

Search engines, YouTube, Reddit, and most social platforms are optimised for engagement and advertising, not knowledge transfer. The algorithm detects interest in a topic and floods the feed with adjacent content — reviews, listicles, personalities, hot takes — whatever keeps attention on the platform longest. Foundational material loses this competition: it is slow, dense, and does not reward infinite scrolling.

The practical consequence is that typing a topic into a search bar usually returns the most SEO-optimised surface, not the most useful source. The noise is not a bug in the system; it is the system working as designed.

## Going Upstream

The antidote is to stop searching directly and instead go to sources one level above the algorithmic feed:

- **Wikipedia bibliographies and citations** — often the fastest way to find canonical texts in a field. Skip the article body; read the references.
- **archive.org** — for out-of-print foundational books, old manuals, and primary source material that the web has forgotten.
- **Recommendations from long-standing practitioners** — people who have done the thing for years tend to cite a small overlapping set of resources. Find that set.
- **Old canonical books** — written before the incentive to optimise for engagement existed. Less noise per page.

The Whole Earth Catalog is a useful model here: it was explicitly a filter, compiled by someone with taste, pointing at tools and books that worked. Any domain benefits from finding its equivalent.

## Triangulation

No single source is unbiased, including foundational ones. The move is not to find the one true source but to triangulate across several:

- Two or three trusted anchors in different formats (a book, a practitioner's notes, a reference manual)
- Cross-check what they agree on — that is the durable part of the knowledge
- Notice where they disagree — that is where nuance lives

Over time this builds filters. You learn to smell marketing, to recognise when a resource is selling a framework versus describing a reality, to tell a first-principles explanation from a cargo cult one.

## Practice as the Final Filter

Consumption alone cannot tell you which ideas are load-bearing. The final filter is practice: build something, hit the edges of what you know, and let the problems you run into tell you which resource actually helps. Resources that answer real questions earn their place. Resources that only sound good in the abstract fall away.
